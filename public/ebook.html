Introduction — How This Study Started

I first encountered Pornography when I was around 15 years old. At the time, it felt normal and insignificant. Over the years, it became a recurring habit—sometimes more present, sometimes less, but always part of my routine.

For a long period, this behavior followed a repetitive pattern of attempts to change and eventual relapse. Even when I managed to stop for short periods, the underlying cycle remained unchanged.

What became clear was that common advice and surface-level explanations were not enough to explain why the behavior persisted.

This study focuses on understanding the internal mechanisms behind this habit: how it forms, how it repeats itself, and what sustains it over time.

Core Question of the Study

The main question behind this entire study was simple:

Why is pornography so hard to quit compared to other habits?

  • People quit alcohol.
  • People quit cigarettes.
  • People quit sugar, games, social media.

But pornography feels different.

It feels more persistent, more internal, more resistant to willpower.

I suspected that the problem was not lack of discipline, but lack of understanding.

The 90-Day Experiment

At a certain point, I decided to run a structured experiment on myself.

For approximately 90 days, I exposed myself to Pornography under controlled conditions, deliberately altering a key aspect of the usual pattern.

The objective was not pleasure.
The objective was observation.

This is where it became clear what needed to change in order to reduce the behavior and reclaim a sense of normal life—without carrying persistent guilt or self-judgment.
